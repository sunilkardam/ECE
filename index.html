Key Findings

Structural shortage, not aberration: Nationally, over 30% of children who need care lack access. The gap exceeds 50% in Nevada, Hawaii, North Carolina, and Alaska.
Single-parent catastrophe: In Massachusetts and California, childcare costs consume 62% and 50% of single-parent income respectively — functionally pricing them out of the workforce.
Rural compounding: Rural gaps consistently exceed urban gaps in 38 states. Nevada's rural gap (67%) nearly doubles its urban counterpart — signaling supply deserts beyond city centers.
Workforce crisis feeding itself: Care workers earn a national median of ~$12–13/hr, driving chronic turnover that further constrains supply — a feedback loop suppressing both workforce participation and care quality.
Fertility as a leading indicator: Every state saw fertility rates fall from 2015 to 2023. States with the highest care costs correlate with the steepest fertility declines — a long-run workforce and fiscal risk.
